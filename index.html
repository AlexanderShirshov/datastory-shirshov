Data Collection made by Alexander Shirshov

Determining the main topic of data collection was an easy step as I had previous interest in understanding how much time I spent reading about Formula 1 in general, and if my reading habits reflected my interest in my favorite team within the sport overall.

With the topic determined, I had to figure out an easy way to track my reading habbits. This was eventually done through a screen capture app on my phone combined with on screen notes. Each time an article was read, a screen capture was taken when first openign the article and when reaching the bottom. Notes were writen on the later screen capture for that article. These notes denoted in short hand how long the article took to read and if continued on to another article from the suggestions shown below. The before and after screen capture helped determine how long it took me to read, with other information also being part of the image including date and time.

Data iteself was organized as seen on right. Conditional formatting was used to help see key aspects at a glance (e.g. whether the article was read, what team it was mainly about) and comments were included for all variables collected (denoted in column names at top).

Data Visualization

The visualization on the right displays my reading habbits of Formula 1 stories as agregated form the data collection described above. It uses a set of differing and multi layerd (in meaning) marks in combination with visualization of time before and after a race event.

Marks are differet for irepresenting related news read before, during and after a Formula 1 (F1) race. Marks are patterned different for whether an article was read, skimmed, or simply viewed in abstract. Further, marks are coloured to denote the Formula 1 team that the news story largely focused on.

Visually, the marks are designed to look like air currents as they flow around the race car at high speeds. In wind tunnel testing of cars, smoke is often used to demonstrate the air efficiency of a car. here, the marks change shape to represent whether they are before the air hits the car, while its flowing smoothly along side, and as it ruffles behind it. Of course, this shape denotes if the news story was read before, drign or after as well, previously discussed.

To aid in identifying the marks and adding more granualarity to how many days before and after a race a story was read (for an two maximum days before and after any particular race day) areas before and after the car are noted in grey boxes. these grey boxes are designed to look like the edges of the race track as seen in some photos.

Astroturfing Explained Video

Out group chose Astroturfing as the topic of our explained video. None of us had any previous experience in learnign about what it was nor did we know of how it may affect ourselves and others in everyday life.

Astrotufing can be easily defined as spreading fake reviews on the internet. For a more complex definition can be found on dictionary.com, paraphrased as a deceptive tactic for creating fake grassroots movements with a specific interest in shaping public opinion about a particular topic in one direction or another.

The structure of the explain video was as follows:

  • Scene 1 - Overall introduction to the video topic
  • Scene 2 - General explanation of astroturfing
  • Scene 3 - Examples of astrotufing both hypothetical and in the real world
  • Scene 4 - A tangential topic explanation of cyberturfing, a modern equivalent of astrotufing
  • Scene 5 - Examples specific to cyberturfing that relate to everyday internet users
  • Scene 6 - Conclusion and review of topics covered

    • In the end, our motivation for understand the topic helped us in the initial stages of creating the explained video. We had well put together research and ended up with a problem of too much information to be put into the format of a short video. After several revisions to our script we were boiled it down to the key facts.
